Final Report - Predicting the Outcome of NBA Games

1. Introduction/Background

As many sports bettors understand, successful betting requires a combination of luck and knowledge. While playing games of chance, knowing the odds as best as one can (and hopefully better than others who bet) will likely lead to positive returns. So, while luck remains beyond our control, enhancing our knowledge of the odds is a valuable proposition. In the NBA regular season, 30 teams each play 82 games, for a total of 1230 games. In the postseason, the playoffs are an elimination tournament consisting of 15 best-of-seven matchups, leading to a maximum of 105 playoff games and a minimum of 60. Each game involves a number of quantifiable events and statistics that together can indicate the likely outcome. So in total, a gambler has at least 1290 opportunities to make money betting on the outcome of NBA games in a year.

The dataset that we intend to use is a collection of games played from 2004 onwards.

Dataset:

https://www.kaggle.com/code/nathanlauga/nba-games-eda-let-s-dive-into-the-data

2. Problem Definition

Given an upcoming NBA matchup between two teams, how is a person to pick a likely winner? When there’s a superteam, like the 2016 - 2019 Golden State Warriors, in the match, a regular person could be assumed to be right more than half of the time (if they pick the superteam). But what about when the matchup is more even? Our project hopes to answer the question of which NBA team will win a matchup with as much accuracy as possible.

A more interesting problem, beyond just the binary win-lose, is: can a person predict the final scores in a game with reasonable accuracy? This is also the subject of many bets on bookkeeping websites, and so is another opportunity for enterprising gamblers to make money.

3. Methods

Data Preprocessing:

Data Cleaning

The original dataset has a number of irrelevant or redundant features.The majority of these columns correspond to some form of non-numerical data, though some of those would almost certainly be useful when building a larger, more complex model. Following the removal of these features, we extracted the columns corresponding to the ground truth labels (Home Team Wins, Home Team Points, and Away Team Points). After the removal of all of those, we were left with a dataset with 10 features.

PCA

Once we had those 10 features, we implemented PCA, choosing to retain 95% of the variance which reduced the number of features in our dataset from 10 to 4.

ML Algorithms:

Logistic Regression

The first ML algorithm that we have implemented is logistic regression. Because logistic regression works well with binary classification problems and our prediction only has two possible outcomes (which team wins), we found it natural to make use of logistic regression as a simpler start to our use of ML algorithms.

SVM

The second ML algorithm that we implemented is an SVM. We chose this particular method because SVMs are inherently binary classifiers, fitting nicely with our problem. In addition, SVMs were mentioned in the paper by Zhao, Du, and Tan as a method used in the prediction of the outcome of basketball games [2].

CNN

The last ML algorithm that we implemented is a CNN. CNNs are not inherently binary classifiers like Logistic Regression and SVMs, however like SVMs, neural networks are mentioned in the paper by Zhao, Du, and Tan as a popular method for game outcome prediction [2]. The same paper makes use of something called a Graph Convolutional Network, and so we thought it would be interesting to see if a CNN could achieve a higher level of accuracy, despite the problem not being one of image classification [2].

Our implemented methods can be found on our github repo in the Jupyter Notebook and PCA code files:

https://github.gatech.edu/jcranor6/MLGroup30

4. Results and Discussion

Between our use of PCA and logistic regression, we have come to a 75.3% accuracy for predicting the winner of a basketball game, given the statistics of the two NBA teams. While our current accuracy is short of the 80% goal that we previously set, it still sits near other projects that rely primarily on logistic regression, which generally have accuracies of 70% to 75% [2].

The accuracy of the SVM method was very close to the accuracy of logistic regression, also sitting at 75.3%. Interestingly, this is notably better than 60% to 64% that other projects achieved using SVMs, though obviously with a different dataset [2].

The accuracy of CNN was the worst of the three, sitting at 74.7%. This is in line with our expectations, as both logistic regression and SVMs are methods better suited for this type of binary classification. It is worth noting that even as our least accurate model, the CNN does better than most of the methods in the paper by Zhao, Du, and Tan, though it is tough to identify exactly why that might be [2].

Visualizations: 

PCA:

Throughout the six different PCA comparisons we can see some decently clear groups of data that are going to work well for the logistic regression. Below we can see how the logistic regression handled the PCA.

Logistic Regression:

In these visualizations, this is where we can see why our success rate is around 75.3%.  (Those that are yellow on the yellow side of the graph are a success, same with purple, and if a purple or yellow is on the wrong side it is counted as a failure)

This is because in most of these comparisons we can see two distinct groups between the yellow and the purple. Specifically the PCA comparisons that did the best were the PC2 vs PC4 and PC2 vs PC3. These comparisons visually appear to have the best success rate. Arguably, why we missed our goal of 80% is because of the PC1 vs PC3 comparison. This graph performed by far the worst as we can see the line is not even remotely close to the middle of the circle of points.

From this point on, we want to make some changes to try to get the success percentage up to our goal of 80%. Using Logistic Regression and PCAs has been effective but we plan to move towards using SVMs and Neural Networks to try to increase the accuracy. By finding out which of these 4 options produces the best results, we can showcase that in the final model.

SVM

The SVM as stated above performed very similarly to that of logistic regression, with both around a 75% accuracy. In the graphs above, however, we can see slight differences in how they performed when handling PC1 vs PC2. The logistic regression decision boundary has a positive slope while the SVM has a negative slope. While they might have their slight differences both were able to create pretty accurate decision boundaries. Furthermore, both algorithms struggled with forming the decision boundary for PC1 vs PC3. In both the decision boundary does not help in deciphering the points. Due to the fact that both algorithms struggled with the specific PC1 vs PC3 comparison it might reflect a problem in the original processing.

When diving deeper into the visualizations, we find that SVMs had a much larger runtime. The logistic regression was able to produce very similar results for a much shorter runtime. This suggests that although they performed similarly in predicting the accuracies of our games, that logistic regression would be able to produce results at a much larger scale, and is therefore a better algorithm from our results.

CNN

For the above graphs, it appears as though the CNN had some apparent problems which warranted it the lowest accuracy. In the model accuracy graph, the validation accuracy started to decrease which could  represent overfitting and also dropped underneath the training accuracy for a period of time. This is not ideal and could demonstrate that the model is not generalizing well to the data. Furthermore, in the model loss graph the validation has a period where it increased, representing that it could be overfitting. Ideally you want the training loss and training accuracy to be in sync and it does not appear so.
